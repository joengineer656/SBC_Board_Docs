The board exposes a standard 40-pin (2×20) GPIO header that gives you access to general-purpose I/O lines, power rails, and dedicated peripheral interfaces. Pins are arranged in two parallel rows running the length of the header, with **odd-numbered pins on the inner row** (closest to the board edge) and **even-numbered pins on the outer row**, starting from pin 1 at the end closest to the board's USB connectors.

<Warning>
  All GPIO pins operate at **3.3 V logic levels**. Never connect a 5 V signal directly to any GPIO pin — doing so will permanently damage the SoC. If you need to interface with 5 V devices, use a dedicated level-shifting module between the device and the header.
</Warning>

## Pin Numbering

There are two common ways to refer to GPIO pins, and mixing them up is a frequent source of bugs:

- **Board mode (physical numbering)** — Pins are numbered 1 through 40 based on their physical position on the header. Pin 1 is marked with a small triangle or square pad on the PCB. This is the numbering used in the tables below.
- **BCM / GPIO mode** — Pins are referred to by the GPIO number assigned by the SoC (e.g., `GPIO17`). This is the numbering used by most Linux GPIO libraries and frameworks such as `libgpiod` and many Python hardware libraries.

Always confirm which numbering scheme your software library expects before wiring a circuit. The table below lists both so you can cross-reference without ambiguity.

<Tip>
  Always place a **330 Ω series resistor** between a GPIO output pin and the anode of an LED. This limits current draw to a safe level (~6–10 mA) and prevents the GPIO driver circuit from being damaged by excessive load. For multiple LEDs, use a dedicated driver IC or transistor array.
</Tip>

## Special Functions

The GPIO pins share circuitry with several dedicated peripheral controllers. Enabling one of these functions in software typically makes the pin unavailable for general-purpose use at the same time.

<Accordion title="SPI Pins (SPI0)">
  The board exposes one hardware SPI bus on the 40-pin header:

  | Signal | GPIO # | Physical Pin |
  |--------|-------:|-------------:|
  | MOSI   | GPIO 10 | 19          |
  | MISO   | GPIO 9  | 21          |
  | SCLK   | GPIO 11 | 23          |
  | CE0    | GPIO 8  | 24          |
  | CE1    | GPIO 7  | 26          |

  Enable SPI in your OS configuration before using these pins with an SPI library. On most Linux distributions, this means enabling the appropriate SPI device tree overlay in your boot configuration file (e.g., `spi0` or `spi-gpio`). The bus supports clock speeds up to several tens of MHz in practice, but the usable maximum depends on your wiring length, capacitance, and peripheral datasheet.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="I2C Pins (I2C1)">
  The primary I2C bus used by HATs and user peripherals is **I2C1**:

  | Signal | GPIO # | Physical Pin |
  |--------|-------:|-------------:|
  | SDA1   | GPIO 2 | 3            |
  | SCL1   | GPIO 3 | 5            |

  GPIO 2 and GPIO 3 have **on-board 1.8 kΩ pull-up resistors** to 3.3 V. You generally do not need to add external pull-ups unless you have many devices on the bus.

  **I2C0** (GPIO 0 / GPIO 1, pins 27 / 28) is reserved for the HAT ID EEPROM. Avoid using it for general devices unless you have disabled the EEPROM scanning feature.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="UART Pins (UART0)">
  The primary UART is available on the 40-pin header for serial console access or communication with external devices:

  | Signal | GPIO # | Physical Pin |
  |--------|-------:|-------------:|
  | TXD0   | GPIO 14 | 8           |
  | RXD0   | GPIO 15 | 10          |

  The default baud rate for the Linux serial console is **115200 8N1**. Disable the serial console in your OS settings if you want to use this UART freely in your own application. Note that at 3.3 V logic, you need a USB-to-TTL serial adapter to connect to a PC — do **not** connect directly to an RS-232 port.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="PWM Pins">
  The hardware PWM controller exposes up to two independent channels on the 40-pin header:

  | Signal | GPIO # | Physical Pin | Channel |
  |--------|-------:|-------------:|---------|
  | PWM0   | GPIO 12 | 32          | PWM Ch 0 |
  | PWM0   | GPIO 18 | 12          | PWM Ch 0 (alt) |
  | PWM1   | GPIO 13 | 33          | PWM Ch 1 |
  | PWM1   | GPIO 19 | 35          | PWM Ch 1 (alt) |

  GPIO 12 and GPIO 18 are both routed to PWM Channel 0 — you can use either, but not both simultaneously as independent channels. The same applies to GPIO 13 / GPIO 19 for Channel 1. Hardware PWM produces much smoother signals than software PWM and is preferred for motor control and audio frequency generation.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="ADC Pins">
  The on-chip analog-to-digital converter is accessible through the following GPIO pins. Each channel provides a **12-bit resolution** reading with a 3.3 V reference:

  | Signal  | GPIO # | Physical Pin |
  |---------|-------:|-------------:|
  | ADC CH0 | GPIO 26 | 37          |
  | ADC CH1 | GPIO 27 | 13          |
  | ADC CH2 | GPIO 28 | —           |
  | ADC CH3 | GPIO 29 | —           |

  Note that GPIO 28 and GPIO 29 are not exposed on the standard 40-pin header but are accessible via test pads on some board revisions. Do not apply voltages above 3.3 V to ADC pins — no over-voltage protection is provided.
</Accordion>
